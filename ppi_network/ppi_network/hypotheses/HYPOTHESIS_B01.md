# Hypothesis B01 — Biological Recovery in Real-World Yeast PPI Benchmarks

## Status: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED

## Background

In [Analysis 01](smairt_template_demos/ppi_network/ppi_network/analysis/ANALYSIS_01.md) and [Analysis 02](smairt_template_demos/ppi_network/ppi_network/analysis/ANALYSIS_02.md), we proved that standard graph theoretic centralities and community detection algorithms perfectly recover planted nodes and modules, and maintain high performance under random edge rewiring noise up to 30%. In this third iteration, we transition to real biological data (Fidelity Ladder Level 3 / Track B). We evaluate whether our validated pipeline can identify biologically essential yeast genes as hubs and accurately partition functional protein complexes.

## Hypothesis Statement

**Prediction**:
When our graph-theory pipeline is applied to a real-world Yeast PPI subnetwork consisting of high-confidence physical interactions (confidence score >= 700):
1. **Hub Recovery (Essentiality)**: The top-3 proteins ranked by degree and betweenness centrality will be enriched for known essential yeast deletion-project genes, achieving a Precision@3 >= 0.66.
2. **Community Recovery (Complexes)**: Modularity community detection will accurately cluster proteins into their respective curated biological complexes (Ribosome, Chromatin, and Cytoskeleton), achieving an Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) >= 0.70 and Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) >= 0.70.

**Rationale**:
- **Hubs**: Essential yeast proteins tend to lie at crucial topological hubs within cell-cycle, translation, and skeletal networks; therefore, centrality rankings (both local degree and bottleneck betweenness) will align strongly with knockout lethality.
- **Communities**: High-confidence physical interactions are highly modular because they form physical multi-subunit complexes (such as ribosomes and nucleosomes) that function as singular mechanical entities, creating a strong modularity signal that greedy modularity can easily extract.

**Success criteria**:
- Precision@3 >= 0.66 for Hub Recovery (identifying essential yeast deletion-project genes).
- Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) >= 0.70 for Community Recovery (comparison against curated biological complexes: Ribosome, Chromatin, Cytoskeleton).

## Experimental Design

- **Script**: `experiments/02_downloaded/script_B02_yeast_benchmark.py`
- **Phase**: downloaded
- **Track**: Track B (Real Biological Data)
- **Data**: Curated Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) PPI subnetwork downloaded from STRING or fallback local resources.
- **Controls**: Comparison with random background associations.
- **Key metrics**:
  - Precision@3 and Recall@3 against known yeast essential proteins.
  - Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) and Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) against curated protein complex labels.

## Dependencies

- `data/downloaded/yeast_ppi.csv` generated by `script_B01_download_yeast_data.py`.
- Shared library: `scripts/shared/logging.py` for `TeeLogger` and `setup_logging`.

## Results

The hypothesis was **partially supported**:
- **Hub Recovery**: Refuted. Precision@3 was 0.33 for both degree and betweenness centrality, as non-essential histones (*HTB1* and *HHO1*) ranked high due to their dense structural complex connections. This shows topological centrality is not a perfect proxy for biological essentiality.
- **Community Recovery**: Supported. Modularity partitioning successfully separated proteins into their respective complexes (Ribosome, Chromatin, Cytoskeleton), achieving an ARI of 1.0000.

See [`analysis/ANALYSIS_B01.md`](smairt_template_demos/ppi_network/ppi_network/analysis/ANALYSIS_B01.md) for full details and visualizations.

## Notes

- In real biological systems, communities can be overlapping; however, physical complexes are typically discrete and provide an excellent testing ground for hard partitioning.
